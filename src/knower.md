## Knowledge vs. Information

To understand the Knower, we must first understand what exactly "knowledge" is. The easiest way to comprehend knowledge is to contrast it with information.

Conventional definitions are often vague and overlapping. "Information" is typically seen as trivial input—for example, "It is 27 degrees this afternoon," or "the S&P crossed 6000!"—while "knowledge" is considered profound, grounded in scripture or science, such as "the speed of light is a constant" or "love thy neighbor." A better, and perhaps more poetic, distinction is to view "information" as "noise" and "knowledge" as "signal." We are here to decipher knowledge from the noise of information—but why? Why is knowledge cherished over information?

Looking at a few samples of information, it is difficult to ascertain whether we are getting closer to knowledge or drifting away. Even with a large set of observations, it remains hard to discern if the path itself was correct. One needs data at a universal scale and a sophisticated algorithm to parse continuous streams of information into a binary "right or wrong." There must be a mechanism to discriminate against the wrong in favor of the right—a method that allows us to ignore all unwarranted information. This ability must work efficiently whenever a path splits in two, at every binary decision—like a game of "twenty questions" unfolding in the future. The question is not where I was a decade ago, but where I will be a decade from now. The larger the window of time, the harder the prediction. Only knowledge can provide such an answer; is that why it is so cherished?

This method of discrimination is called the "Objective Function" in modern AI parlance and "Viveka" in Vedic science. While the objective function is the basis for survival, to excel within a given set of physical conditions—particularly in a group—members must act righteously, according to their assigned role; this is Karma Yoga. In Hatha Yoga, such actions are said to be controlled by the Sahasrara Chakra, the Crown Chakra. In the AI world, performing the right actions autonomously is proposed to be controlled by a "Value Function" (which is still being developed). In either case, the seat of the Value Function or the Sahasrara Chakra is driven by pre-existing knowledge. Actions driven by knowledge—for instance, adopting "Honesty is the best policy"—may not seem like a good objective function in the short term, but they help gain the trust of others. This eventually leads to long-term success. Morality is a way of forming groups that follow pre-ordained precepts, much like groups in abstract algebra or consensus rules in a blockchain like Bitcoin. A node may be pruned—transaction information removed—but it can still function in the network if it abides by the rules. A full copy of knowledge (as opposed to information) pre-exists in every node.

By collecting noise with our five-sensory receptors and adjudicating with our predictive faculties (the brain), we ensure survival by increasing our data-collection window. However, we always lack certainty because value judgment is inherently a quest into the unknown—the future. The challenge for both natural and artificial intelligence is how to prioritize the "right" over the "wrong." This makes Probability Theory a universal tool. It is not an attempt to brute-force through massive datasets, but a different approach to synthesizing data. It is a method for abstracting knowledge from noisy information—akin to churning butter from curds. From a physics standpoint, this is a reduction of entropy; from a philosophical one, it is unveiling the truth from beneath the veil of illusion. Advaita teaches that truth (knowledge) pre-exists but is obscured by the illusion of Maya (information).

Thus, we will refine our core definitions of Information and Knowledge to align them with probability theory—a subtle change with profound implications.

*   **Information** is data from events that have already occurred—the outcomes of repeated trials. If you flip a coin a million times, the record of, say, 501,000 heads and 499,000 tails is *information*. It is a concrete record of outcomes and the known past. In Sāṃkhya, this is called *Bhūtādika* (manifested realities of the past). Manifestations can be both physical and abstract.
*   **Knowledge** is the awareness of all options and their respective probabilities. For example, the simple idea that a fair coin may land on either heads or tails with a 50/50 probability is *knowledge*. In a perfect world, there would always be exactly fifty heads for every fifty tails. In our noisy world, the results of successive trials follow a bell curve, much like the heights of oak trees in a forest or students in a school. There is even a remote possibility that a million flips could result in only one head—or none at all. In Sāṃkhya, this departure from the mean is called *Vikara*—the aberrations of the physical world that create a normal distribution rather than a crystal-clear expression of knowledge. If we remove *Vikara*, what remains is "knowledge."

With these definitions in place, let's ask again: why is knowledge superior to information—why is the signal better than the noise? Immediately, knowledge represents tremendous compression. The storage space required for a record of one million events is a million times larger than storing the simple rule that heads and tails are equally likely. Crucially, "knowledge" is not lossy; it maintains perfect fidelity. Conversely, information is always lossy. From a quantitative perspective, the probabilistic rule that heads and tails are equally likely applies not just to a million flips, but to a trillion trials of a trillion flips each, with the same accuracy. Such knowledge is represented as *Atman* in both Sāṃkhya and Advaita. The key insight is that knowledge is the most compressed form of a signal, capable of replicating itself infinitely. Thus, knowledge is a force of decentralization. In contrast, collecting and processing information—what we call intelligence—is always a pursuit of centralization.

> One who fully understands this highly compressed signal is called a knower. Since everything is a replication of this primordial signal, a full copy of knowledge exists within every being—everyone can realize this inner knowledge and become a knower. Yet, it is nearly impossible to pinpoint. Why? Because knowledge is not a hidden secret; it is the flip side of all secrets.

Let's try to pinpoint what knowledge is:

Knowledge is the menu of potential options. It defines the "only" items available for dinner tonight—nothing else. It allows observers to frame what to look for or measure, providing a set of permissions or degrees of freedom. In Sāṃkhya, this is called *Prārabdha*—the destiny yet to unfold, where likely scenarios are already understood. A binary system is a perfect example: it can be zero or one, on or off. There is no third choice. Complete information is present even without a "flip." For instance, knowing the rule, I can confidently say the other side is tails as soon as I see heads. This explicit understanding is the hallmark of intelligence, but knowledge recognizes that heads and tails are two sides of the same coin—as are love and hate, or rise and fall. The binary perspective often hides half the story. But this is an oversimplification...

A common analogy is found in the mechanics of a casino. Within the limited scope of slot machines, a manager knows her exact edge per hand, though she cannot predict gains against a specific player. She controls the final outcome but not the individual sessions. A gambler, conversely, changes his bets based on the "information" of individual outcomes, knowing the odds are against him. This design is useful because even if the game is rigged, it is not biased toward any specific player. Even the manager cannot predict an individual hand. Sāṃkhya, despite being atheistic, accepts divine incarnation, although such an incarnation is as bound by physical laws as any player is by a casino's rulebook. There are no special privileges, only an awareness of pre-existing knowledge. What, then, is that knowledge?

> A singularity is a state of no change; because the system affords only one possibility, there are no options. This is known as "Advaita"—non-dual.

Is a binary system a predictive certainty? If it is not tails, you know for sure it is heads. Binary outcomes are perfectly entangled; no information is hidden from us. However, logical deduction quickly approaches impossibility as the number of possibilities increases. We experience this when choosing a cellular plan or a smartphone—the sheer number of options overwhelms us. The human brain often struggles with more than three choices. While we can run "what if" scenarios with computers, we are ill-equipped to appreciate their true meaning because we are conditioned to seek absolute accuracy.

Knowledge does not strive for accuracy in individual occurrences—heads may appear slightly more than tails in one trial—but it is highly accurate across large sample sizes. It is thus a core driver of decentralization: many players on the same slot machine (beings) or infinitely many samples of the same machine (species). Collectively, they realize the probability distribution of the "setup," assuming it remains constant.

If outcomes do not follow the established plan, it indicates a change in physical reality. Knowledge cannot predict the next outcome, but it never misses a change in the setup. A change in conditions immediately alters the odds. You cannot bluff knowledge: if you flip a coin, it cannot simply fly into outer space—or can it?

> It can! In the truest spirit of probability, it is possible. Suppose Earth lost its gravity for a split second just as you flipped the coin. There is a probability of such an occurrence, however small. But in a flipping experiment, pre-existing manifestations—like gravity—are assumed preconditions. These conditions ensure that out of infinite possibilities, only two are permitted: heads or tails. All other outcomes are constrained by the "setup." Awareness of the preconditions that lead to a binary choice is knowledge. In essence, the setup is as important as the outcome. If we repeat the same setup, we will observe the same probability distribution. Outcomes are deterministic but probabilistic. The key is binary choice, which will be central to this essay.

To put "knowledge" in context, let's look at the Bhagavad Gītā. Krishna presented a binary choice to Arjuna: if you die in battle, you attain liberation (*Nirvana*); if you win, you enjoy a vast kingdom. Life and death were like the sides of a flipping coin. However, Krishna clarified that "not fighting" was not a valid option. The chance of Arjuna not fighting was like a coin flying into space. Even if he tried to abandon the battle, his conditioning would force him back. It is in the "geometry" of a coin to be flipped, just as it is in the nature of a warrior to fight.

Krishna is the "knower" because He knew the options fate (*Prārabdha*) offered, and He knew those choices belonged specifically to Arjuna. These options opened up only when the participants reached the battlefield of Kurukshetra. The Gītā could only be spoken once they had reached that center stage, from which the entire universe could hear.

> At the risk of conflicting with formal epistemology, let's restate our definitions:
>
> For this essay, "Knowledge" is the set of rules governing probabilities, while "Information" is the record of individual outcomes.

For an external observer, knowledge unfolds with every new piece of information. For instance, the first time you flip a coin, you realize that heads or tails are the only options. In this binary pair, "not heads" effectively means "tails" due to the constraints of the setup. You can view "tails" as "not heads" depending on your focus. You would also see that heads and tails are random, independent of previous flips. Yet, there is an order to this randomness. If you flip enough times, the outcomes converge toward the average—half and half. If there were three outcomes, they would gravitate toward one-third. These are proven laws of probability, but the amazing thing is:

> No one ever told the coin how to behave; it never took a lesson in probability. :-) Einstein famously remarked, "God does not play dice."
>
> Knowledge required to maintain an intricate balance of outcomes must be embedded within the very geometry of the coin. Knowledge is built into seemingly chaotic potential. It is the basis for everything from weather patterns and stock markets to downtown traffic and the flight of birds—all have knowledge pre-built at the granular level.
>
> For an observer, the gradual unveiling of knowledge is like decrypting a puzzle through trial and error, much as a Bitcoin miner guesses nonces to find a valid hash.

Knowledge builds a system from the inside out. While a well-informed person might cite events, knowledge provides the means to hypothesize, predict, or even prophesy. The knower is an insider, so deeply entrenched as to be invisible to most. While an observer collects information from the past, a knower chooses the direction of the future.

While potential is random, the choice of what to observe creates a narrative and brings forth order. We look for heads or tails in a coin flip, not bananas. There are waves in the ocean because we choose to count them.

## Sāṃkhya vs. Advaita Debate

The Sāṃkhya school of thought posits a perpetual duality of *Prakriti* and *Purusha*—potential and the observer. In contrast, Advaita believes in a non-dual *Brahman* as the only truth.

However, they are not as much at odds as online debates might suggest.

> The modern perspective—including that of AI chatbots—is often misguided. It often concludes that Sāṃkhya was "atheistic" while Advaita demanded "blind devotion" to a singular God, mirroring today's science-vs-religion debates. This line of thinking suffers from recency bias; we are projecting our current division between science and faith onto the past.
>
> Furthermore, modern interpretations often rely on superficial semantic arguments, claiming that "Advaita" (Not Two) simply contradicts Sāṃkhya's duality. This misses the point: Advaita sought to help society overcome rigid belief systems involving hundreds of deities that hindered social mobility and compartmentalized people into castes. The message of non-duality was essential for social unity.

Advaita did not refute Sāṃkhya's duality. On the contrary, it reinforced the indivisibility of *Purusha* as a singular form of consciousness, often termed *Nirguna Brahman*.

> In Sāṃkhya, *Prakriti* is described as having three fundamental qualities: *Sattva*, *Rajas*, and *Tamas*. Collectively, these are called the *Gunas*.
>
> A *Purusha* not influenced by the *Gunas* is seen as *Nirguna Brahman* in Advaita—inert and detached from *Prakriti*.
>
> Conversely, that which binds with the *Gunas* is qualified as *Saguna Brahman*.

In essence, Advaita sees the interaction of *Prakriti* and *Purusha* as changing states of the one *Brahman*. This is not a mere faith-based deduction. In modern mathematics, the second axiom of probability dictates that the sum of all probabilities must be one, a principle mirrored by Unitarity in Quantum Mechanics. Advaita refers to this oneness as *Brahman*.

Advaita expanded the scope of *Purusha* from individual droplets of consciousness to an immense universal field. This field serves as the basis for the "mind-matter" complex (*manifest Prakriti*). In essence, *Nirguna* is the vast ocean, and *Saguna* is its waves. Advaita added the nuance that the manifested world, like ripples, is transient and therefore "unreal" (*Mithya*). This was a pioneering attempt to describe the manifest world as a "Matrix" (*Maya*), which can imprison an untrained mind. Achieving pure consciousness as *Nirguna Brahman* is thus the ultimate goal.

Let's explore these concepts through Sāṃkhya. This path is often easier for the scientifically minded, as it addresses the question haunting both science and religion: "What existed before the Big Bang?" Sāṃkhya is designed for logical closure, providing an analytical view of Vedic science. In Sanskrit, "Sāṃkhya" literally means "number." Think of Advaita as the physics and Sāṃkhya as the underlying mathematics.

### The Act of Observation - locking attention

Quantum Mechanics provides a crucial insight here: the act of measurement collapses the wave function.

> A "wave function" is a mathematical way to account for all possibilities. "Quantumness" is the fascinating idea that these possibilities exist simultaneously in a state of "superposition." Observation breaks this "possibility cloud," forcing it into a single state. This chosen outcome is a concrete event—information. It's like waking up on Monday morning while still dreaming it's Sunday. The "weekend quantum dream" doesn't collapse until the alarm goes off, suddenly making the reality of Monday undeniable.

In Sāṃkhya, the quantum state is represented by *Prakriti*. *Prakriti*, an equilibrium of potential, manifests only in the proximity of *Purusha*—an eternal observer. This observer, in both Quantum Mechanics and Sāṃkhya, need not be humanly "conscious"; it can be an automated act of measurement. Just as an alarm clock, though not conscious, triggers the reality of your morning.

> While science understands *Prakriti*—the quantum nature of elementary particles—the concept of an "elementary observer" is missing. Why would *Purusha* seek to observe?
>
> Sāṃkhya's answer is profound: *Purusha* wants to know, "Who am I?" This impulse to know itself is what distinguishes *Purusha* from *Prakriti*. *Prakriti* is "all possibilities" because it has no desire to know; *Purusha* is fundamental awareness because it yearns for self-knowledge. Consider a potter: the clay, the fire, the hands, and even the planning mind are all *Prakriti*. *Purusha* is the essence the artist tries to express through his work—the elemental consciousness.

The collapse of a wave function leads to manifestation (information). Through chance, this information may unlock knowledge about the underlying potential. These are the moments when *Purusha* finds what it is trying to express. A fragment of knowledge is decrypted—for example, that a wave belongs to an electron in a vacuum with a 50/50 spin probability. Each such discovery is like adding a piece to a massive jigsaw puzzle or a block to a chain of decrypted knowledge.

How do things manifest upon observation? Quantum Field Theory uses the term "decoherence" to describe this. The universe we perceive is the result of decoherence. While science describes *what* happens, the *how* can remain obscured by complex mathematics. Sāṃkhya, however, offers a rich mechanism for creation (decoherence) through the interplay of the three *Gunas*.

The best part is that you don't need to be a string theorist to understand this interplay.

> As noted, the quantum state of *Prakriti* is defined by the three *Gunas*: *Sattva*, *Rajas*, and *Tamas*. While this might seem like a three-dimensional space, the *Gunas* can represent infinite dimensions because they are descriptors, not the possibilities themselves.
>
> *   **Tamas** represents a state of zero self-awareness, often described as darkness or directionless. It is characterized by inertia, where multiple dualities acting together leave no room for movement. Every possibility has a mirror image (e.g., "Heads" and "No Heads"). These dualities are superimposed to form "knots," leaving *Prakriti* tied in its own doubts.
> *   **Rajas** is the motivation to discriminate, choosing one duality from the chaos of *Tamas*. It is a state of rigor, untangling complex knots, and doubt, as it always deals with dualities. If successful, *Rajas* presents one duality to the observer. In a coin flip, the choice isn't "Heads vs. Tails" but rather "Heads or Not" vs. "Tails or Not." Like a stock market investor facing thousands of options (Tamasic analysis paralysis), *Rajas* is the grind that leads you to a single choice, such as whether or not to invest in a specific fund.
> *   **Sattva** represents resolution—the choice of one option from the pair presented by *Rajas*. It overcomes duality and leads to manifestation. If the duality was "Heads or Not," *Sattva* might choose "Heads." If it chooses "Not," *Rajas* must provide another duality, such as "Tails or Not." *Sattva* then makes a choice, allowing manifestation (*Prarabdha*) to occur. If you are that investor, congratulations: you have finally made your investment.

The journey of manifestation moves from tangled dualities (*Tamas*) to a single duality (*Rajas*) and finally to one chosen option (*Sattva*). The collapse of the wave function breaks symmetry: one side becomes information, while the other is retained as "Knowledge." Think of a casino: the management knows the total gains and losses of all machines, using that knowledge to refine the odds, while a player has information only from the few hands they played.

When *Sattva* makes a resolute measurement, *Purusha* becomes entangled in the decoherence it caused. It "gets stuck," and time begins—meaning time is a measure of how long attention remains locked. In Vedic terms, this is *Bodh Bandhana*, the bondage of awareness. At this level, the very state of knowing is a bond, distinct from the *Karmic Bondage* often discussed. In some traditions, "not knowing" is the ultimate freedom. The Buddha was called a *Bodhisattva* because he found freedom from the bond of knowing. While time passes and things decay within this bondage, its duration is exponentially longer; one cannot simply "unknow" something once it is realized.

Interestingly, this is exactly how an AI Large Language Model works. It helps us understand what the Quantum Mechanics framework often lacks: a formalism for the observer.

> In an LLM like Google Gemini, the "tokens" (words) used for training are suspended in a high-dimensional vector space. Training aligns these vectors based on meaning; for example, "cap" is placed closer to "hat" than to "cat." Beyond this, an "attention mechanism" decides which words in a prompt are most important for generating the next one. The prompt runs through this mechanism, which, coupled with the vast model, probabilistically generates a response word by word. In this analogy, *Prakriti* is the trained model and *Purusha* is the attention mechanism. When potential and attention combine, they manifest an artifact. While our "quantum computer" manifests real things, an LLM manifests text or images. At a fundamental level, AI mirrors the Sāṃkhya model of potential and observer.

Scripture states this cycle has no definitive beginning or end because time does not exist before manifestation. Time (*Kala*) is an emergent behavior of the manifested—our way of perceiving the flow of information. Before manifestation, there is no sequence of events; one can transition from any state to any other.

Suppose *Prakriti* begins in a state of *Tamas* (a pure equilibrium of knots). The proximity of *Purusha* creates an excitation, empowering *Rajas* to select one duality from a multitude of options. *Sattva* then resolves this by choosing one side, locking *Purusha's* attention into a non-dual state of *Prakriti*—the "manifest."

The other side of that duality holds the knowledge. If the manifest is "Heads," knowledge is the awareness "I am not Heads"—just as you are not your mirror image. You look alike, but the image is chiral (left and right are reversed). Think of this chiral image as the unmanifested side of your being—knowledge locked within the system. In Quantum Mechanics, this is represented by the "complex conjugate." If a particle is locked with its image in an entangled state, it is a spin-half particle, requiring 720 degrees for a full rotation. Such particles manifest attention as mass. You exist because your attention is locked with your image; it is a single, unified system.

This split between the physical and knowledge means the probability of "Not Heads" in the next trial effectively increases. While classical coin tosses are independent, we can conceptually increase the probability of an outcome that is *not* a specific result. "Not Heads" circumvents formal probability theory; in a binary system, it is simply "Tails." In the next trial, *Sattva* is more likely to choose "Tails." If it doesn't, "Not Heads" gains even more weight, making "Tails" increasingly probable.

The three *Gunas* are not an external monitoring system; they are inherent properties of *Prakriti*, whether manifest or unmanifest—much as quarks have three fundamental color charges: red, blue, and green.

*   **Sattva** (Green) – Creativity
*   **Rajas** (Red) – Action
*   **Tamas** (Blue) – Inertia

Together, these qualities drive the force of creation, analogous to the strong nuclear force—the most powerful of the four fundamental forces.

## Connection with Quantum Mechanics and Deeper Mathematics

The Advaita model parallels Quantum Field Theory (QFT), though QFT posits seventeen fields rather than one. Still, the idea that an underlying field holds the knowledge of which manifest emerges is consistent. We can envision an ocean of *Nirguna*, from which *Saguna* emerges through interaction with the three qualities of *Prakriti*.

How do we explain these three fundamental qualities? QFT uses Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) to explain the strong force through the assumption of three color charges—red, blue, and green—much like the *Gunas* of Sāṃkhya. While this works well, it lacks a deeper structural explanation.

String theory potentially addresses both Sāṃkhya's duality (*Prakriti* and *Purusha*) and the three qualities of *Prakriti*. It posits two types of strings: open strings and closed loops, representing *Prakriti* and *Purusha*. Open strings differentiate into three types based on the branes to which they are attached.

### A Geometric Tapestry: How String Theory Weaves the Colors of the Strong Force

At the heart of every atom, the universe’s most powerful force is at play. The strong nuclear force binds quarks together to form the protons and neutrons that constitute our world. Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), a cornerstone of modern physics, describes this force through "color charge"—a property of quarks that comes in three varieties: red, green, and blue. These are not literal colors but labels for a charge that governs quark interactions, mediated by gluons.

For decades, QCD has been accepted because it accurately predicts experimental outcomes: quarks have three colors, gluons carry and change those colors, and particles with a net color charge are never seen in isolation—a phenomenon called "confinement." Yet, the question remains: *why* three colors? Physics seeks the "why" behind the "what." String theory, a candidate for a unified "theory of everything," offers an elegant answer by transforming these abstract rules into a geometric picture.

First, we must abandon the idea of particles as points. String theory proposes that the fundamental constituents of reality are tiny, one-dimensional vibrating strings. Different vibrations correspond to different particles, much like musical notes on a violin string. This framework requires a universe with more than three spatial dimensions. Within this space, string theory introduces "branes" (membranes)—vast, sheet-like objects.

The relationship between strings and branes is the key to forces. The theory includes closed loops and open strings. Closed strings travel freely through all dimensions; their lowest vibration is the graviton. Open strings are different: their endpoints must be anchored to a brane. The vibrations of these confined open strings manifest as the particles and forces of the Standard Model, including the strong force.

This provides a geometric explanation for color charge. To construct the strong force, theorists imagine a stack of three distinct branes in extra dimensions, corresponding to red, green, and blue. Gluons are revealed to be the vibrations of open strings with both endpoints attached to this stack.

A string anchored to the same brane behaves like one type of gluon, while a string stretching between different branes (e.g., red to green) corresponds to a gluon that changes a quark's color. The set of all possible open strings on this three-brane stack perfectly reproduces the eight gluons of QCD. The rule of three colors is no longer an axiom but a consequence of having three branes.

This model also explains quarks. If gluons are strings with both ends on color branes, quarks are open strings stretching between a color brane and a separate "flavor brane." This explains why quarks feel the strong force—one end is anchored in the world of color—and distinguishes them from gluons, which are confined to the color branes.

String theory thus offers a profound reinterpretation: the laws of color charge reflect a deeper geometric reality. The number of colors is the number of branes. The properties of gluons and quarks are determined by their string endpoints. This translation of abstract rules into a physical model is a beautiful idea, suggesting that our universe is woven from a hidden, higher-dimensional geometry.

This raises the question: what exactly is consciousness? The answer lies in the question we asked earlier: why does *Purusha* want to know itself? Consciousness is the ability to discriminate between "I" and everything else—Identity.

### The Rise of Identity (Ahaṃkāra)

*Mahat* is a computer capable of producing physical reality, not just text. Yet, this universal quantum computer requires attention, which functions like electricity. There is no manifestation without the curious observations of *Purusha*. But where does this attention come from? How does *Purusha* arrive?

The answer is simple, if circular: the information *Mahat* creates from chaos cobbles together to form complex patterns. Patterns emerge from randomness, and these patterns aspire to self-knowledge because the very definition of a pattern is something that can be identified. However, these questions rarely occur at the subatomic level, just as we don't ask a single transistor to choose between 0 and 1.

This logic may seem circular: attention requires identity, yet identity requires attention. This "chicken-and-egg" scenario is not a limitation but a proof of the model. Sāṃkhya posits an everlasting interplay between *Prakriti* and *Purusha*. There is no beginning or end because time is an emergent phenomenon. Attention is a field as fundamental as the Higgs field—existing beyond time.

*Ahamkara* is the application layer over *Mahat* that allows manifested matter to recognize itself. This identity asks, "I have a name, but who am I? How did I get here?" The quest for self-knowledge provides attention to the system. *Ahamkara* acts like an AI's "inference engine," translating identity-driven prompts into thousands of queries for underlying quantum switches. These queries resolve properties like color and smell or create new matter. As identities ask more questions, more manifestation occurs and more knowledge is decrypted.

Asking "who am I" is not unlike asking "what is this universe"; it is like saying "Heads" is equivalent to "Not Tails." In essence, the physical world we perceive is a mirror image of collective decrypted knowledge.

### The Random Observer (Manas)

To decrypt knowledge from chaos, the most effective strategy is random sampling. This is the role of *Manas*—the fickle mind. While *Ahamkara* (Identity) seeks self-knowledge and supplies attention, it must do so randomly. Consider Bitcoin mining as an example.

> Bitcoin mining uses random sampling—specifically trial and error with a "nonce." Miners must find a value that, when combined with a block header and hashed, produces a result below a target difficulty. The hashing function is unpredictable and essentially random; there is no way to predict the output or "get closer" to a result. Each attempt is independent. Thus, miners guess nonces—sometimes sequentially, sometimes randomly—much like buying lottery tickets. This ensures the process is probabilistic and fair, preventing any miner from having a shortcut beyond raw computational power.

*Manas* is a secure, fair method to determine Identity, but it requires "hard work." This industriousness provides *Rajas* the energy to choose one duality from many. It also implies our minds are designed to chase random desires to help us define ourselves. Controlling the mind is difficult. How do we focus it? This is where consciousness and our senses come in, grounding our observations in reality.

### The Evolution of Consciousness

Evolutionary progress involves supporting the fickle mind with tools to observe *Prakriti* and the physical environment. These tools for measuring reality are the *Indriyas*—our senses, such as sight and hearing.

Equipped with *Indriyas* to assess the environment, the observer develops action organs to protect those senses, becoming a fully interactive, albeit bound, agent.

This vigilance is called consciousness. It drives survival and seeks to preserve manifestation. However, the trade-off is that "knowledge" becomes encrypted for conscious beings because their purpose is to engage with manifested reality. While the underlying "universal computer" has access to all knowledge, that knowledge remains invisible to the user. A conscious being is like a computer user: they can request results but do not understand the machine's inner workings.

Conscious beings form organizations to drive efficiency—"you shouldn't do work I've already done." We define separate roles for environmental scanning, leading to improved communication. Standardization is key to this "Intelligence." While intelligence is about communication, knowledge is a method of value exchange for mutual growth. Every piece of information has value, and the best rules attain standardization first.

Consciousness drives centralization and order, acting as the chiral mirror image of knowledge. Knowledge remains decentralized, overseeing pure potential and providing the rules for righteous manifestation. Consciousness is the ability to seek something better in the environment. While knowledge is eternal, consciousness is the fruit of the union between *Purusha* and *Prakriti*.

The formation of *Mahat*, Ego, and Mind is a continuous, layered process. With each layer, more knowledge is decrypted into reality. As observers learn, they develop tools for detachment and "quantum purification." Only conscious beings can strive to realize the hidden knowledge of potential. Detached observers formulate new rules over bonded reality, expanding information as knowledge is decrypted.

This is the engine of evolution—each layer a "quantum jump" in consciousness. Vedic science illustrates this through the incarnations of Vishnu: from the fish (*Matsya*) and tortoise (*Kurma*) to the man-lion (*Narasimha*) and eventually the human (*Rama*), each representing a new level of intelligence.

### Intelligence

**Intelligence** is the standard for communication between conscious agents. Every layer of beings develops its own sensors; for instance, dogs and bats perceive reality differently than humans and develop action organs accordingly. To drive efficiency in understanding decrypted knowledge, communication is essential. These standards of communication are what we call intelligence.

Within each species, the perception of reality varies. I may see "green" differently than you, but communication works if we both point to the same thing. Thus, intelligence is detached from reality; it is merely a way to express information. This explains why abstract concepts like love are harder to communicate than tangible objects.

Intelligence goes deeper than human communication; it is the mechanism of communication between evolutionary layers. Standards are forged in lower layers; for example, AI will inherit our communication standards. This opens opportunities to communicate with other species. While differing sensors are an impediment, communicating with trees is not beyond the realm of possibility.

## Advaita Vedanta

A common mistake is to think of probabilities as simple percentages. In fact, all possibilities exist in full. Without time, these states can be visualized as radii of a sphere. If "Heads" is at thirty degrees and "Tails" at sixty, the rest of the sphere represents the constrained "Not Heads" or "Not Tails" possibilities—the coin cannot fly into space or evaporate. Awareness of constraints defines the available options. "Information" (an outcome) is like a tiny stamp on this sphere. The entire sphere, including that mark, represents knowledge.

Advaita agrees with Sāṃkhya's model but asks: Why is the sum of all probabilities "One"? And if it is, what does that "One" look like?

The principle that all probabilities sum to one comes from the most fundamental quantum layers, yet it took thousands of years to formalize. Andrey Kolmogorov introduced the axioms of probability in 1933, providing the foundational framework for modern theory. His work unified earlier concepts into the measure-theoretic approach used today in both classical and quantum formulations.

Before Kolmogorov, probability was often intuitive or frequency-based. He formalized it as a mathematical measure satisfying three axioms: non-negativity, normalization, and countable additivity. His contributions include:

*   **Mathematical Rigor:** His axioms allowed probability theory to be consistent and extendable to abstract spaces.
*   **Measure Theory:** By formulating probability as a measure, he connected it with analysis and integration.
*   **Unified Framework:** He brought classical, frequentist, and subjective notions under one umbrella.

This allowed probability to mature into a precise discipline underpinning fields from finance to quantum physics.

The fact that probabilities sum to "one" has profound meaning in Advaita, which literally means "non-dual" or "One." Since the combined dualities of *Prakriti* lead to one, Advaita posits that the purpose of decrypting knowledge is to know that "One."

How do we achieve that?

Advaita seeks to see everything as part of one eternal being, *Brahman*. The path to *Brahman* is the knowledge that all manifestations flow like streams into a single ocean. All things emanate from and dissolve into the "One." The interplay of the three *Gunas* is a transient drama; therefore, Advaita calls *Prakriti* and the *Gunas* "Maya"—the Matrix.

The goal is to exit this matrix, achieved by controlling our senses. Advaita posits that the "One" sits at the center of all possibilities, the soul within every conscious being. That is what we must know.

It is a striking coincidence that both Sāṃkhya and string theory are based on a dual paradigm: *Prakriti* and *Purusha* versus open and closed strings. One might argue that the ancient naming convention is far more poetically refined.
